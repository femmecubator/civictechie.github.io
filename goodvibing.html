Project Plan

GOOD VIBING: In search of meaningful alternatives to #civictech building in the Age of AI

Is vibecoding justifiably good for the Civic Tech community?
Version 1.5.0 30-Day Experiment Civic Tech Community

Project Overview

Good Vibing is a 30-day civic tech experiment that critically examines whether vibecoding — using large language model tools to build software with minimal traditional coding — can be a force for good in the civic technology space.

The experiment does not begin from a position of optimism. Our design manifesto is clear: designing with LLM tooling could be inherently unethical. We embark on this study to search for good alternatives, and to document honestly what we find.

Core Question

Can civic technologists participate in and leverage vibecoding tools for good — or do all paths lead to the harmful scenarios we have already predicted?

The Problem We Are Investigating

Vibecoding sits at the intersection of several urgent tensions in technology today:

  • Data centers are being built out at scale to support a trillion-dollar LLM industry, with enormous environmental costs.
  • Ethical AI policy and legislation are trailing far behind the speed of deployment.
  • The technology is already being misused — malvertising via vibecoded sites, unauthorized use of writers' and designers' work, and accelerating narratives about the diminishing value of human creative labor.
  • Designers and community builders have historically been locked out of building tools without technical collaborators. Vibecoding changes that equation — but at what cost?
Our Stance

We believe tech solutioning often yields bad results. Good designers focus on the problem first and ask whether a tech solution is even needed. Maybe analog is better.

Experiment Focus Areas

Participants will concentrate their 30 days across three civic domains:

Domain 1 Domain 2 Domain 3
Build Tools for Education Tools for Democracy Designing for Civic Change

Participants choose one domain and pursue a real project within it, using small language models (Mistral or Bloom Science) rather than large commercial models wherever possible.

Implementation Phases

Phase 1 Foundation Days 1–5
  • Recruit 5–10 civic technologists, designers, and community builders as participants
  • Run an orientation session covering guiding principles, tooling setup, and ethical framework
  • Each participant selects a project and completes a written proposal (#proposalphase)
  • Establish shared documentation channels (video diary format, open-source repo)
  • Baseline measurement: document current collaboration dependencies and skills
Phase 2 Build Sprint Days 6–20
  • Participants work independently using approved small language models
  • Daily or every-other-day video diary entries documenting progress, frustrations, and surprises
  • Weekly group check-ins to challenge first drafts and share findings (#goodvibing)
  • Track state of each project: #proposed → #ideated → #implemented
  • Document environmental and resource costs as they become visible
Phase 3 Evaluation Days 21–27
  • Participants complete evaluative testing on their own work using the shared rubric
  • Scorecard completion for each project against the two core criteria: Serves Humanity and Sustainable
  • Loss and gain accounting: document what was gained (digital prototype) and what was lost (direct collaboration, etc.)
  • Aggregate data across all participants for pattern analysis
Phase 4 Design Crit and Synthesis Days 28–30
  • Host a public design critique with reviewers of diverse expertise
  • Share video diaries and project outcomes openly
  • Synthesize findings into a publishable report
  • Issue a final verdict on the central question: Is vibecoding sufficient to set up future tooling for civic change?

Guiding Principles

Every decision made during this experiment is governed by the following principles:

Start with Tooling

At the outset, we are choosing to use small language models such as Mistral AI or Bloom Science. We also plan to build our own Small Language Model for Civic Tech.

Second-Order Thinking

Before proceeding with any build decision, participants ask: Why does this benefit the community? What is the real cost of not doing this work? Is the harm of doing it greater than the harm of not doing it?

Choose Evergreen

All work produced is made reusable and available to the broader community. No one should need to repeat this effort. Outputs are designed to be maintained and built upon, not abandoned.

Challenge the First Draft

With an open-source mindset, we invite other builders to review, question, and push the work. No output is treated as final. Other builders are welcome to join #goodvibing activities and contribute critiques.

Definition of Success

This experiment does not measure success by whether vibecoding is vindicated. Success is defined as:

  • Honest, documented findings that the community can build on
  • At least one project reaching #implemented status
  • A reusable rubric and scorecard available to other civic tech builders
  • A published report that contributes to the ethical AI conversation from a designer-first, community-first perspective
  • Clarity on whether this path is worth continuing — or a well-reasoned case for why it is not

Evaluation and Scoring

Framework Good Vibing

Conduct Evaluative Tests

What is the rubric? Can we develop a way to measure the environmental impact of GoodVibing?

Design a data index for evaluation:

  • Sustainable solutions
  • Craft and skill
  • Economic labor
  • Cognitive sovereignty
  • Mission-centricity

Create a Scorecard

Define our scorecard and data index around two core criteria:

  • We are looking to create a system of scoring work based on drafted areas: socio-cultural context, craft and skill, economic labor, cognitive sovereignty, mission-centricity.
  • Low-fidelity artifact could be a simple checklist or a way to assess an implementation plan md file. If builders proceed to vibecode a static site, we can ask them to complete the quiz, and share an approximation of a total impact score.
  • We can also measure by using and deploy guiding principles such as second order thinking etc. to help the user make better choices. (ie: Is it better tooling, encouraging more collaboration or providing opportunities to enlist freelance support to show how labor upskilling outweighs the speed of building.)

Environmental Impact Measurement

Measuring the environmental cost of vibecoding is a core part of this experiment, not an afterthought. We aim to develop a reusable methodology for civic tech builders assessing the carbon and resource footprint of LLM-assisted work.

  • Track model size and inference calls per project
  • Estimate compute cost using available emissions calculators
  • Compare against equivalent human-collaborator workflow
  • Publish findings as part of the Evergreen output so others can replicate the measurement

Write the Narrative

Framework Good Vibing

1. Write the Narrative

Three Honest Questions

Q1 Is there hope for this tool to be used for good?
Q2 Is there a meaningful alternative to using it?
Q3 Does the good outweigh the bad?

Impact Phase Tracking

Each project is tracked across three stages. Impact at each stage is assessed differently:

#proposed #ideated #implemented
  • #proposed — Value of the idea as articulated. Is the problem real and worth solving?
  • #ideated — Value of the concept as designed. Is the solution coherent and community-informed?
  • #implemented — Value of the thing as shipped. Does it reach people and create change?

Loss and Gain Accounting

Each participant documents a qualitative ledger at the close of the experiment. These are not presented as cancelling each other out — they are held in tension.

Lost
  • Direct human collaboration
  • Reliance on embodied expertise
  • Certain forms of creative ownership
Gained
  • Digital prototype
  • Speed of iteration
  • Access to building without a technical partner
